--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TASK OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This project falls under the domain of Geospatial Data Analysis — a branch of
data science that focuses on understanding patterns, distributions, and
relationships in data that has a geographic component (i.e., latitude and
longitude coordinates).

The dataset contains information about 9,551 restaurants spread across 15
countries and 141 cities worldwide. Each record includes the restaurant's
exact geographic coordinates, its city, country, cuisine type, average cost,
and most importantly — its aggregate rating from customers.

The task is broken into three key analytical goals:

  1. Visualize restaurant locations on a map
  2. Analyze how restaurants are distributed across regions
  3. Determine if a restaurant's location correlates with its rating

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PART 1 — VISUALIZING RESTAURANT LOCATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WHAT WAS DONE:
  Every restaurant in the dataset has a latitude (north–south position) and
  a longitude (east–west position). These two numbers pinpoint its exact
  location on Earth's surface.

  Using the Folium library (a Python tool built on top of Leaflet.js), two
  types of interactive maps were created:

  (a) CLUSTERED MARKER MAP (interactive_map.html)
      - Each restaurant is represented as a colored dot on the map.
      - The color of the dot reflects the rating:
          Dark Green  = Excellent (≥ 4.5)
          Green       = Very Good (≥ 4.0)
          Light Green = Good      (≥ 3.5)
          Orange      = Average   (≥ 3.0)
          Red         = Below Average (≥ 2.0)
          Gray        = Unrated
      - Nearby markers are automatically grouped into clusters to avoid
        visual overload. Clicking a cluster zooms in and expands it.
      - Clicking any individual marker shows a popup with the restaurant's
        name, city, country, rating, and cuisine.

  (b) DENSITY HEATMAP (heatmap.html)
      - Instead of individual points, this map shows "heat" concentrations.
      - Areas with many restaurants glow red/yellow; sparse areas appear blue.
      - This clearly reveals geographic hotspots of restaurant density.

  A static scatter plot (fig4_geo_rating_map.png) was also produced, where
  longitude is plotted on the X-axis, latitude on the Y-axis, and each point
  is color-coded by its rating (Red = Low, Green = High). This provides a
  quick visual overview of the global rating landscape.

WHY IT MATTERS:
  Visual maps make spatial patterns immediately apparent. A data table cannot
  convey at a glance that almost all restaurants are concentrated in South
  Asia — but a map makes this obvious within seconds.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PART 2 — DISTRIBUTION ANALYSIS ACROSS CITIES AND COUNTRIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WHAT WAS DONE:
  After mapping the locations, the distribution of restaurants was analyzed
  statistically and visually.

  COUNTRY-LEVEL DISTRIBUTION:
  - India dominates the dataset with 8,652 restaurants (over 90% of all records).
  - The United States comes second with 434, followed by the UK (80), Brazil (60),
    UAE (60), and South Africa (60).
  - This heavy skew toward India means the dataset is not globally balanced,
    which is an important caveat for interpretation.

  CITY-LEVEL DISTRIBUTION:
  - New Delhi alone has 5,473 restaurants — more than all other countries combined.
  - Other major Indian cities (Gurgaon: 1,118 and Noida: 1,080) follow.
  - International cities like Abu Dhabi, Cape Town, and Singapore appear with
    relatively small counts (20–60 each).

  RATING PATTERNS BY COUNTRY:
  - A boxplot shows the spread of ratings in each country.
  - A bar chart shows the mean rating per country.
  - Indonesia and the Philippines consistently show higher median ratings,
    while some countries (e.g., Brazil, Turkey) show more variability.

  TOP-RATED CITIES:
  - Cities with at least 10 restaurants were ranked by their average rating.
  - Smaller cities in Southeast Asia and the Middle East often outperform
    major Indian metros despite having far fewer entries.

WHY IT MATTERS:
  Understanding distribution is fundamental before drawing any conclusions.
  If the data is heavily concentrated in one region, any "global" finding must
  be interpreted with caution. This analysis makes those biases transparent.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PART 3 — CORRELATION BETWEEN LOCATION AND RATING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WHAT WAS DONE:
  A statistical test was conducted to determine whether a restaurant's
  geographic coordinates (latitude and longitude) have any linear relationship
  with its aggregate rating.

  METHOD USED: Pearson Correlation Coefficient (r)
  - r ranges from -1.0 to +1.0
  - r = +1.0 → perfect positive relationship (as one increases, so does the other)
  - r = 0.0  → no linear relationship
  - r = -1.0 → perfect negative relationship
  - A p-value < 0.05 indicates the result is statistically significant.

  RESULTS:
  - Latitude  vs Rating:  r ≈ -0.07  (p < 0.05) — Significant but very weak
  - Longitude vs Rating:  r ≈ -0.04  (p < 0.05) — Significant but very weak

  INTERPRETATION:
  Both correlations are statistically significant due to the large sample size
  (nearly 9,000 rated records). However, the effect sizes are extremely small
  (|r| < 0.10), meaning that latitude and longitude explain less than 1% of
  the variation in ratings.

  In plain language: knowing only a restaurant's coordinates tells you almost
  nothing about how it will be rated by customers.

  The weak negative sign on latitude suggests that restaurants in regions
  closer to the equator (lower latitudes, like Southeast Asia) tend to have
  marginally higher ratings — but this is likely explained by regional
  cultural dining standards and local rating behavior, not geography itself.

WHY IT MATTERS:
  This finding is valuable because it rules out a simplistic geographic
  explanation for rating differences. Future analysis should examine factors
  like cuisine type, price range, and local competition as stronger predictors
  of restaurant rating.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
CONCLUSIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  1. The restaurant dataset is geographically concentrated in India, making
     global comparisons possible only with appropriate context.

  2. New Delhi is the single largest restaurant hub in the dataset.

  3. Southeast Asian and Pacific cities (Indonesia, Philippines, New Zealand)
     lead in average customer ratings.

  4. Geographic coordinates (latitude and longitude) show a statistically
     significant but practically negligible correlation with restaurant ratings.

  5. A restaurant's location does not directly determine its quality score.
     Cultural expectations, cuisine category, and pricing are likely stronger
     drivers of rating variation.

  6. Recommendation: Incorporate cuisine type, price range, and regional
     benchmarking in future analyses for a more complete picture.
